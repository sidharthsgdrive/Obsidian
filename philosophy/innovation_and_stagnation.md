> [!tldr] TL/DR
>Lack of innovation in anything is an indicator as well as a cause of decay. 

***
I finished reading *Prelude to Foundation* a few days ago, and one of, if not the, main idea presented in the book is the decay of the Empire characterized by a singular lack in innovation and scientific progress.
I find the thought personally relevant, in that my diary system in Obsidian has kind of stagnated or settled to an equilibrium, without any big changes. As a result, or maybe as a cause, I find myself skipping entries every few days, which is rather unusual.
***
It seems only natural that with a drop in interest for something, one does not feel like changing the system much, and adopts an **"If it ain't broke, don't fix it"** mentality, which is practically understandable, but unsustainable for maintaining long-term commitment.
What if the sameness and absence of novelty works to reduce one's interest in the field, in a sort of **feedback loop**?
I further propose that this is the basic mechanism by which one loses interest in a pursuit, passion, or hobby. When you stop expanding your knowledge in the field, when you stop trying to change the way you approach the hobby, you stop thinking consciously about what you're doing, and come to view those actions as routine chores.
This may also be generalized for one's whole life. When you stop caring about improving, about learning, about inspecting and modifying the foundational way you do things, you lose interest in life.
