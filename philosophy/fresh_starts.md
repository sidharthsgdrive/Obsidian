`14/12/25`
For a long time, I've had this habit where I consider some events as a "fresh start", where I can resume or improve on major pursuits. I think, to some extent, this is human nature, as displayed in the tradition of New Years' resolutions. 
For me, it's usually twice a year, conveniently spaced somewhat evenly apart; those being January 1st, and May 19th, my birthday.
Theoretically, these "starts" are supposed to bring on a period of productivity, but I often find that's not the case either. However, I do think psychologically it is a good thing.
Why don't these starts always blossom into productivity? Perhaps it's because of undue pressure to improve, at the time.

An idea I've had for a while is that the optimal course of action would be to narrow down the interval between these fresh starts, to increase the average proability density of improvement, so to speak. 
How would I go about doing this? It seems like the first of a month is a good starting point. After all, I've created [monthly notes](monthlynote_template.md) for that purpose.
One downside of the monthly interval is that there is often no clear event to mark the beginning and end of a month.

A narrower interval would be weekly. Under this system, I would consider Mondays as fresh starts, for obvious reasons. The feeling you get on a [Sunday evening](philosophy/sunday_evening.md) is common, and as devastating as it is relatable when you see it on a meme.
This interval is more tangible, as it is apparent when you have a day off, followed by a day of work.

Even narrower would be a daily interval. Would this be optimal though?
One principle I haven't quite written down is that of [moderation](philosophy/moderation.md). I feel like attempting to start every single day better than before could lead to oversaturation of the feeling, which would cause disgust and abandonment of the very idea of improvement. 
In [innovation_and_stagnation](philosophy/innovation_and_stagnation.md), I said:

> [!quote] Innovation and Stagnation
>  What if the sameness and absence of novelty works to reduce one's interest in the field, in a sort of **feedback loop**?

I admit I've lost my thread of thought on how to approach this problem. I'll revisit it with the solution some other time.
