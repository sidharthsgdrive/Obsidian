I feel sometimes that there are so many barriers that the brain constructs to prevent me from seeing the world as it is.
Sometimes, I get a horrible realization that I am not just a part of this world, I am the observer, and that everything happening around me is seen, heard and felt through me. When I am gone what will be left? Nothing, because the observer is gone.
In those moments, it feels so strange because it feels like the normal identity which I have is an abstraction constructed to distract me from the fact that I am me, not just a random person but the person who is perceiving the world.
In [[the_plurality_of_identity]], I thought about how the mind assumes various identities in different contexts. I think the identity assumed in these moments of almost Lovecraftian horror is one of detachment, insofar as it is drastically different from the normal ones.
Why is this identity assumed less often than the others? Could it be because of the increased stress, leading to internal resistance against assuming it? I don't think so, because it's not so drastically uncomfortable. Maybe I'm so caught up in mundane activities that I don't often get to reflect that deeply, that seems more likely.
