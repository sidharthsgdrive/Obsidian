2. John von Neumann
	* John von Neumann was a Hungarian-American mathematician, physicist, computer scientist and engineer. He was a founding figure in the field of computing, with significant contributions to hardware design, theoretical computer science, and the philosophy of computer science. 
	* Though the single-memory, stored-program system is commonly called von Neumann architecture, it is actually a misnomer. While consulting for the EDVAC project, Neumann wrote a draft report on the system, which nullified the patent claims of the actual inventors Eckert and Mauchly, before they published their work.
3. von Neumann architecture characteristics
	* In this architecture, data and instructions are stored in the same memory, which allows for more flexible, general-purpose computing as opposed to the previous fixed program computers (e.g. calculators)
	* Some attributes of von Neumann architecture are as follows: 
		* Memory (RAM) for storing instructions and data
		* a Central Processing Unit (CPU) which includes an ALU and Control Unit
		* Input and Output (I/O) mechanisms
		* Sequential instruction processing
4. Impact of Von Neumann architecture on computing
	* The stored-program concept made computers more flexible, easier to program and capable of multitasking.
	* This allowed for general-purpose computation (reprogramming computers for different tasks without modifying the hardware)
	* The Von Neumann model is the basis for modern CPUs, which follow the basic structure of a CU for managing instruction execution, an ALU for mathematical operations, and RAM for storing data and instructions.
5. Evolution of modern computing systems
	* The development of superior technologies laid the foundations for huge improvements in performance and compactness of computers.
	* Each generation of computing is defined by a characteristic technology which formed the basis of its hardware.
6. First Generation (Vacuum Tube) computers
	* A vacuum tube is a device that controls the flow of electric current through a high vacuum so that an electrical potential is applied.
	* Computers made using this technology occupied a lot of space and were massive.
	* Programming had to be done using very low level languages (machine language)
	* Some examples for first generation computers are ENIAC and its successor EDVAC.

8. Second Generation (Transistor) computers
	* The main change in second generation computers was the use of transistors in the place of vacuum tubes.
	* As we know, transistors are semiconductor devices used to amplify or switch electronic signals. They can be made much more compact than vacuum tubes.
	* Additionally, computers made using transistors use less power and generate less heat, and are more reliable than their vacuum tube counterparts.

10. Second-Generation languages (Assembly, Fortran, COBOL)
	* Second-generation programming languages were made to provide a layer of abstraction on top of machine code, to make it a little more human-readable.
	* Assembly language, a processor-dependent low level language, is just a mapping of human-readable symbols to opcodes, addresses, numeric constants, and so on.
	* One machine instruction is represented as a line of assembly code, commonly called a _mnemonic_.
	* Some slightly higher level languages such as Fortran and COBOL were developed in this era, to simplify Assembly programming.
11. Third Generation (IC) computers
	* As computers got more complex, it became harder and harder to reliably manufacture CPUs using individual transistors.
	* In 1958, Jack Kilby and Robert Noyce developed the Integrated Circuit or IC, which is a semiconductor material which includes thousands of miniaturized transistors.
	* The computers manufactured using ICs were even faster, and more compact and reliable than those of the previous generation.
	* Magnetic disks and tapes, and later Floppy Disks, were used for storage.

13. High level languages (C)
	* High-level languages have strong abstraction from details of the computer. In contrast to low-level languages, they use natural language elements, and automate or even hide systems like memory management from the user.
	* These features made the process of developing programs more simple.
	* Some languages developed in this period include Pascal, C, and Prolog. Most of the programming languages we use today are high-level.
14. Fourth Generation (microprocessor) computers
	* Starting with the fourth generation, computers began to use microprocessors instead of simple ICs.
	* A microprocessor is made up of thousands of integrated circuits that are assembled on a single chip known as a silicon chip.
	* This generation kicked off the advance of integration technologies, from MSI to LSI to VLSI.
	* With the use of these technologies, it was possible to create computers that were smaller and more powerful than ever before, including the first personal computers.
15. Moore's Law
	* In 1965, American businessman and engineer Gordon Moore noted that the number of transistors per integrated circuits had been doubling every year, and predicted that this rate of growth would continue.
	* To this day, this empirical relationship holds true. However, a consensus has not been reached on when Moore's law will cease to apply.
16. GUIs
	* Before the advent of GUIs, users interacted with computers using the Command-Line interface (CLI). This approach, though still powerful, is less intuitive for non-technical users.
	* With the development of personal computers in the fourth generation, it was necessary to overcome this problem. Thus, the introduction of GUIs made computing more intuitive by using icons, windows, menus, and a mouse for navigation.
17. Fifth Generation computers - The Present Day
	* Computers today still use the microprocessor system, but the limits of scaling are yet to be reached with current ULSI (Ultra Large Scaling Integration) technology.
	* Some areas emphasized in the fifth generation of computing are parallel processing and Artificial Intelligence.
	* With the development of emerging technologies such as Quantum Computing, there may yet be another paradigm shift in the way we develop, manufacture, and interact with computers.
